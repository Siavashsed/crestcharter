The 10 Percent Rule That Kills Most Private Jet Purchases

Practical finance tips for people who charter jets, villas and yachts, from someone who has run the ownership math and lost money learning it.

If you fly fewer than about 200 hours a year, chartering almost always beats owning, and the gap is wider than any broker will tell you. I learned that the expensive way in 2019, when a client of mine bought a used Citation CJ3 for $4.9 million and asked me to sanity check the budget six months in. His projected annual operating cost was $780,000. Actual, once we counted the two unscheduled maintenance events and the second pilot he had to keep on salary, came to just over $1.1 million. He flew 96 hours that year. That's roughly $11,400 an hour for an aircraft he could have chartered at $3,900.

The pattern repeats across villas and yachts with almost boring consistency. People with real money make good decisions about earning it and surprisingly sloppy ones about spending it on things that float, fly, or sit empty eleven months a year. The financial advice aimed at this bracket tends to be either flattery or fear. What actually helps is running the numbers the same way you'd run them on a business you were buying.

Run the Utilization Number Before Anything Else

Here's the single calculation I'd make anyone do before they sign. Take the realistic annual usage, not the aspirational one. Then divide total annual cost of ownership, including depreciation, insurance, crew, dockage or property management, and a maintenance reserve, by that number. Compare it to the charter rate. If ownership doesn't beat charter by at least 30 percent, you're buying a lifestyle statement, which is fine, but call it what it is.

The National Business Aviation Association puts the breakeven for most light and mid-size jets somewhere between 200 and 240 flight hours annually. Below that, fractional or charter wins. Most private owners I've worked with fly between 80 and 130 hours. They're on the wrong side of the line and they know it by year two.

Yachts are worse. The rule of thumb the brokerage world quotes is 10 percent of purchase price per year in running costs, and in my experience that number is optimistic once you factor Mediterranean crew wages and a proper refit reserve. A $12 million yacht used six weeks a year is costing you somewhere north of $200,000 per week of actual use. You can charter something better for a quarter of that.

Liquidity Beats Almost Every Trophy Asset

The most common mistake I see isn't overspending. It's illiquidity. People tie up capital in assets that take nine to eighteen months to sell, then find themselves cash-constrained during exactly the moments when cash matters most, like a business downturn or a divorce or a market dislocation.

The Federal Reserve Survey of Consumer Finances shows that even in the top wealth decile, a large share of net worth sits in assets that can't be converted quickly. That's fine when it's a business or real estate producing income. It's a problem when it's a depreciating toy with a carrying cost.

My rule, and it's opinionated: never let non-income-producing luxury assets exceed 5 percent of liquid net worth. Not total net worth. Liquid. If you can't write the cheque twice without changing your life, you can't afford it once. I've written more about how this plays out in why most rich people still get finance wrong, and the liquidity trap is the one that catches even sophisticated operators.

The Fee Layers Nobody Itemises

Charter and villa bookings carry cost layers that don't appear until the invoice. On jets, watch for repositioning legs, overnight fees, de-icing, international handling, and the fuel surcharge that floats independently of the quoted hourly rate. A quoted $4,200 an hour on a mid-size can settle at $5,600 all-in. Ask for an all-in estimate with the empty-leg positioning included, in writing, before you book.

Villas have their own version. The nightly rate is rarely the number. Add the mandatory concierge fee, the chef minimum, the security deposit that ties up capital for six weeks after checkout, and the local tourist tax that in parts of Italy and the Balearics has climbed sharply. Budget 25 to 35 percent above headline rate and you'll be about right, most of the time.

  • Jets: ask for the trip sheet with positioning legs shown separately
  • Villas: get the service charge and staff gratuity policy in writing
  • Yachts: the Advance Provisioning Allowance is typically 25 to 35 percent of the charter fee and it is not a deposit, it gets spent

I'll be honest about one thing here. A friend of mine, sharp guy, runs a logistics business, ignored all of this and booked a week in Mykonos through a concierge outfit he found on Instagram. He told me the rate was $38,000. The final invoice was $61,000 and he still isn't entirely sure what the $9,000 "villa management coordination" line covered. He paid it. I would have argued. I'm not 100 percent sure arguing would have worked, given the contract language, but... you should at least read the thing before the plane lands.

Where Tax Logic Turns Into Expensive Fiction

The pitch you'll hear is that ownership pays for itself through depreciation and charter revenue offset. Sometimes true. Often not. The IRS applies real scrutiny to personal use of business aircraft, and the substantiation burden is genuine. If the asset isn't predominantly used for a legitimate trade or business, the deduction thesis collapses and you're left with an aircraft and a penalty.

The charter-back model deserves particular skepticism. Yes, your management company can put the jet on their certificate and generate revenue. That revenue also puts hours on the airframe, accelerates maintenance cycles, and means strangers use your aircraft on your preferred weekends. In the deals I've reviewed, charter-back covered somewhere between 20 and 45 percent of fixed costs, never the whole thing, despite what the projection spreadsheet said.

There's genuine debate here. For a founder flying 300 hours a year to sites where commercial service is bad, ownership plus a management agreement makes real financial sense, and I've recommended it. The Knight Frank Wealth Report tracks steady growth in this bracket for exactly that reason. The distinction is usage intensity, not wealth level.

Today, pull your last 24 months of travel and count the actual hours or nights. Not the ones you meant to take. Divide the full carrying cost of the asset you're considering by that real number, then put the result next to a charter quote for the same aircraft or villa. If the two numbers are within 30 percent of each other, charter and invest the difference. That one afternoon of arithmetic has saved the people I've worked with more money than any other single thing I've told them.

Frequently asked questions

How many hours do you need to fly per year to justify buying a private jet?

Roughly 200 to 240 hours annually for a light or mid-size jet. Below that, charter or a fractional share almost always costs less per hour once you count depreciation, crew salaries and maintenance reserves.

What does it actually cost to run a yacht each year?

Budget about 10 percent of the purchase price annually, and treat that as a floor rather than a ceiling. Crew wages, dockage, insurance and refit reserves push many owners closer to 12 or 14 percent in the Mediterranean season.

Is chartering out your jet a reliable way to cover ownership costs?

It covers part of them, typically 20 to 45 percent of fixed costs in the deals I've reviewed. It also adds airframe hours, accelerates maintenance cycles and means the aircraft may be unavailable on the dates you want it.
